Concept & Design Process

From initial concept to final design: the creative and engineering process behind roller coasters

The concept behind our masterpiece

Big loop into small loop

This was the most effective way in getting 4 points while including loops. Instead of 2 big loops, we picked one big and one small to preserve track length while still getting points for loop size and double loops.

Tiny hill

We found out through trial and error the precise speed for the next part, and we decided to include a small hill to decrease the speed of the marble by a tiny margin.

Twisted turn downwards

The twisted turn downwards allowed for the ball to travel to a lower elevation while maintaining speed and saving tracklength, it also allowed the marble to do a 180 degree turn in the oposite direction.

Drop and catch

This was the peak of our creativity, by throwing the marble off the track and then catching it, we were able to do something completely outside the box and amazing to look at.

Final double spiral

With the remaining speed, energy and track length, we decided to do 3 spirals which counts as 2 points, centrifugal force allowed it to roll down without falling off.

Ball contact

The marble that falls will hit a second marble and fall into the pocket behind while the new marble continues the legacy.

The Physics Behind the Thrills

Energy Conversion

Roller coasters are masterpieces of energy transformation. The excitement you feel on the ride is powered by two main forms of energy: potential energy and kinetic energy. These two types of energy transform, from potential energy at the higher points of the roller coaster, to kinetic energy at the lower points of the roller coaster.

Mechanical Energy

Although potential and kinetic energy transform between different points, mechanical energy stays relatively the same, constant throughout the entire rollercoaster (when air resistance, friction, slope, drag, etc... is considered).

Velocity and Acceleration

Enough velocity allows the marble to pass through sections of the rollercoaster such as loops, spirals, and sharp turns. Without enough the required velocity needed, the marble would not be able clear those obstacles. Acceleration allows the roller coaster to create thrills, mimicing high speeds while keeping the marble secure on the track.

Calculations

  • Steel roller coasters now dominate the market, accounting for over 70% of new installations since 2000.
  • The average height of roller coasters has increased by over 150% in the past three decades.
  • Inversions have become more common, with modern coasters averaging 3-5 inversions per ride.
  • Wooden coasters have seen a resurgence in popularity, with new technologies allowing for smoother rides.
  • Themed roller coasters that integrate with surrounding attractions are becoming increasingly popular.

Trend Analysis

Height vs. Speed Correlation

There is a strong positive correlation between roller coaster height and maximum speed, though the relationship is not perfectly linear due to different design philosophies.

Manufacturer Specialization

Different manufacturers have developed specialties, with some focusing on record-breaking rides while others excel in themed experiences.

Regional Preferences

Different regions show distinct preferences for coaster types, with inversions being more popular in Europe while height and speed are prioritized in North America.
